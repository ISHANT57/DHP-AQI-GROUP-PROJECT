US EPA AQI Basics

The Air Quality Index (AQI) developed by the US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) is a public-friendly, color-coded numerical system ranging from 0–500. It translates complex pollutant concentrations into categories like Good, Moderate, and Unhealthy to help people understand daily air quality risks. It monitors pollutants like PM2.5, PM10, O₃, NO₂, SO₂, and CO.

For more information: US EPA AQI Basics (AirNow.gov)

US AQI (EPA) vs WHO 2021 Air Quality Guidelines

Aspect US AQI (EPA) WHO Air Quality Guidelines (2021)
Type Numerical index (0–500) with color-coded categories (Good, Moderate, Unhealthy, etc.) Concentration-based health guidelines for individual pollutants (e.g. PM2.5, PM10, NO₂, O₃)
Purpose Public-friendly, daily air quality risk communication tool Health-based recommended maximum exposure limits
Categories / Scale ✔️ Yes — 6 categories: Good, Moderate, Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups, Unhealthy, Very Unhealthy, Hazardous ❌ No categories or AQI scale — only specific safe pollutant limits
Pollutants Measured PM2.5, PM10, O₃, NO₂, SO₂, CO PM2.5, PM10, O₃, NO₂, SO₂, CO (same pollutants but stricter limits)
PM2.5 24-hr limit example 0–12 µg/m³ = Good, 35.5–55.4 µg/m³ = Unhealthy 15 µg/m³ maximum (exceeding this is unsafe)
Stringency More lenient; allows higher pollutant concentrations before labeling air as unhealthy Stricter; lower thresholds based on latest health evidence
Official AQI Index? ✔️ Yes ❌ No

Sources: US EPA AQI Basics, WHO 2021 Global Air Quality Guidelines
